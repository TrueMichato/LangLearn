# Common Mistakes by English Speakers (Типичные ошибки англоговорящих)

Every language has traps for foreign speakers, and Russian is no exception. English speakers bring assumptions from their native language — about word order, verb usage, and vocabulary — that lead to predictable mistakes. This lesson covers the most common pitfalls so you can recognize and avoid them.

---

## 1. False Friends (Ложные друзья переводчика)

"False friends" are words that look or sound like English words but mean something completely different:

| Russian word | Looks like... | Actually means | English equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| магазин *(magazín)* | magazine | store/shop | журнал *(zhurnál)* = magazine |
| фабрика *(fábrika)* | fabric | factory | ткань *(tkan)* = fabric |
| симпатичный *(simpatíchnyy)* | sympathetic | attractive, nice-looking | сочувствующий *(sochúvstvuyushchiy)* = sympathetic |
| аккуратный *(akkurátnyy)* | accurate | neat, tidy | точный *(tóchnyy)* = accurate |
| актуальный *(aktuálnyy)* | actual | current, relevant | действительный *(deystvítelnyy)* = actual |
| гениальный *(geniálnyy)* | genial | brilliant, genius | дружелюбный *(druzhelyúbnyy)* = genial |
| реализовать *(realizovát)* | to realize | to implement, carry out | понять *(ponyát)* = to realize (understand) |
| директор *(diréktor)* | director (film) | manager, principal | режиссёр *(rezhissyór)* = film director |

**Я иду в магазин.** *(Ya idú v magazín.)* — I'm going to the store. (NOT "magazine")

**Она очень симпатичная.** *(Oná óchen simpatíchnaya.)* — She's very attractive. (NOT "sympathetic")

**Это актуальная тема.** *(Éto aktuálnaya téma.)* — This is a current/relevant topic. (NOT "actual")

## 2. The Missing "To Be" in Present Tense

In English, "to be" is essential: "I am a student," "She is a doctor." In Russian, the verb **быть** (to be) is **omitted** in the present tense:

### Correct Russian (no "to be")

**Я студент.** *(Ya studént.)* — I am a student. (literally: "I student")

**Она врач.** *(Oná vrach.)* — She is a doctor.

**Это интересно.** *(Éto interésno.)* — This is interesting.

**Москва — красивый город.** *(Moskvá — krasívyy górod.)* — Moscow is a beautiful city.

### Common mistake

❌ **Я есть студент.** — Sounds extremely unnatural.
✅ **Я студент.** — I'm a student.

❌ **Она есть красивая.** — Wrong!
✅ **Она красивая.** — She is beautiful.

> **Exception:** «Есть» IS used in the construction **У меня есть...** *(U menyá yest...)* — "I have..." to emphasize existence: **У меня есть машина.** — I have a car.

> In past and future tenses, быть reappears: **Я был студентом.** *(Ya byl studéntom.)* — I was a student. **Она будет врачом.** *(Oná búdet vrachóm.)* — She will be a doctor. (Note the instrumental case!)

## 3. Genitive After Negation

English speakers often forget that negation in Russian changes the case of the object:

### "I have" vs. "I don't have"

**У меня есть книга.** *(U menyá yest kníga.)* — I have a book. (nominative)

**У меня нет книги.** *(U menyá net knígi.)* — I don't have a book. (genitive!)

### Common mistakes

❌ **У меня нет книга.** — Forgot to use genitive after нет.
✅ **У меня нет книги.** — I don't have a book.

❌ **У меня нет брат.** — Same error with masculine noun.
✅ **У меня нет брата.** — I don't have a brother.

❌ **У меня нет время.** — Neuter nouns change too!
✅ **У меня нет времени.** — I don't have time.

### More examples

**В комнате нет стола.** *(V kómnate net stolá.)* — There is no table in the room.

**У нас нет денег.** *(U nas net déneg.)* — We don't have money.

**Здесь нет интернета.** *(Zdes net internéta.)* — There's no internet here.

## 4. Motion Verbs: идти vs. ходить, ехать vs. ездить

English has one word "go," but Russian distinguishes between **unidirectional** and **multidirectional** motion:

| Unidirectional (one way, right now) | Multidirectional (round trip, habitual) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| идти *(idtí)* | ходить *(khodít)* | to go (on foot) |
| ехать *(yékhat)* | ездить *(yézdít)* | to go (by vehicle) |
| бежать *(bezhát)* | бегать *(bégat)* | to run |
| лететь *(letét)* | летать *(letát)* | to fly |

### Unidirectional — going somewhere right now, one direction

**Я иду в школу.** *(Ya idú v shkólu.)* — I'm going to school (right now, on my way).

**Он едет на работу.** *(On yédet na rabótu.)* — He's going to work (right now, by transport).

### Multidirectional — habitual, round trips, general ability

**Я хожу в школу каждый день.** *(Ya khozhú v shkólu kázhdyy den.)* — I go to school every day.

**Ребёнок уже ходит.** *(Rebyónok uzhé khódit.)* — The child already walks. (general ability)

**Мы часто ездим в Петербург.** *(My chásto yézdim v Peterbúrg.)* — We often go to Petersburg.

### Common mistakes

❌ **Я хожу в магазин сейчас.** — Right now, one direction → use идти.
✅ **Я иду в магазин сейчас.** — I'm going to the store right now.

❌ **Я каждый день иду на работу.** — Habitual → use ходить.
✅ **Я каждый день хожу на работу.** — I go to work every day.

## 5. Word Order Pitfalls

Russian word order is "free" compared to English, but it's not random. Russian uses word order for **information structure** — what's known (topic) comes first, and what's new (focus) comes last:

### Neutral word order

**Мама приготовила ужин.** *(Máma prigotóvila úzhin.)* — Mom made dinner. (neutral — who + what)

### Emphasis on who

**Ужин приготовила мама.** *(Úzhin prigotóvila máma.)* — It was Mom who made dinner. (emphasizing мама)

### Common English-speaker mistakes

❌ Using rigid SVO order in all sentences when Russian would naturally rearrange for emphasis or flow.

**В парке гуляли дети.** *(V párke gulyáli déti.)* — Children were walking in the park. (natural Russian — location first)

**English instinct:** "Дети гуляли в парке." — This is also correct but emphasizes the children, not the scene.

> **Rule of thumb:** Known/old information first, new information last. Questions answer "what about X?" — X goes first.

## 6. Gender of Borrowed Words

Borrowed words don't always follow standard gender rules, and English speakers often guess wrong:

| Word | Gender | Why it surprises |
|---|---|---|
| кофе *(kófe)* | **masculine** | Ends in -е but is masculine (historically) |
| метро *(metró)* | neuter | Indeclinable — doesn't change form |
| такси *(taksí)* | neuter | Indeclinable |
| кафе *(kafé)* | neuter | Indeclinable |
| пальто *(paltó)* | neuter | Indeclinable |
| виски *(víski)* | neuter | Indeclinable |

**Чёрный кофе.** *(Chyórnyy kófe.)* — Black coffee. (masculine adjective!)

❌ **Чёрное кофе.** — Technically accepted colloquially now, but traditionally incorrect.

**Московское метро.** *(Moskóvskoye metró.)* — Moscow metro. (neuter adjective)

> **Indeclinable nouns** (mostly foreign borrowings ending in -о, -е, -и, -у) don't change form in any case. They're usually neuter, with some notable exceptions like кофе.

## 7. Forgetting Reflexive Verbs

Many common Russian verbs are reflexive (-ся/-сь) where English uses non-reflexive forms:

| Russian reflexive | English |
|---|---|
| учиться *(uchítsya)* | to study / to learn |
| нравиться *(nrávitsya)* | to be liked / to appeal |
| бояться *(boyátsya)* | to be afraid |
| улыбаться *(ulybátsya)* | to smile |
| стараться *(starátsya)* | to try |

### The нравиться trap

English: "I like coffee." → Subject = I, Object = coffee.
Russian: "Coffee appeals to me." → Subject = coffee, "to me" = dative.

**Мне нравится кофе.** *(Mne nrávitsya kófe.)* — I like coffee. (literally: "Coffee pleases to-me")

❌ **Я нравлю кофе.** — This means "Coffee likes me"!
✅ **Мне нравится кофе.** — I like coffee.

**Ей нравятся эти песни.** *(Yey nrávyatsya éti pésni.)* — She likes these songs.

## 8. Other Common Traps

### Confusing "learn" and "teach"

**Учить** *(uchít)* — to teach (someone) / to study, memorize (something)

**Учиться** *(uchítsya)* — to study, to be a student

**Я учу русский язык.** *(Ya uchú rússkiy yazýk.)* — I'm studying Russian. (memorizing)

**Я учусь в университете.** *(Ya uchús v universitéte.)* — I study at university.

### "To know": знать vs. уметь

**Знать** *(znat)* — to know (facts, information)

**Уметь** *(umét)* — to know how to (skills)

**Я знаю его имя.** *(Ya znáyu yevó ímya.)* — I know his name.

**Я умею плавать.** *(Ya uméyu plávat.)* — I know how to swim.

❌ **Я знаю плавать.** — Wrong verb for skills.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **False friends** will betray you — магазин means "store," not "magazine."
2. **Omit "to be"** in present tense — Я студент, NOT Я есть студент.
3. **Genitive after negation** — У меня нет книги, NOT книга.
4. **Motion verbs** — идти (one way, now) vs. ходить (habitual, round trips).
5. **Word order** conveys emphasis — new information goes last.
6. **Кофе is masculine** — чёрный кофе, not чёрное.
7. **Нравиться** flips the subject — Мне нравится кофе = "I like coffee."
