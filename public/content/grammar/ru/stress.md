# Stress in Russian

Word stress (ударение) is one of the trickiest aspects of Russian. Unlike languages with predictable stress patterns (like French or Polish), **Russian stress can fall on any syllable** and must be memorized for each word. Getting stress wrong doesn't just sound odd — it can change pronunciation (due to vowel reduction) and sometimes change a word's meaning entirely.

---

## 1. Why Stress Matters

Stress in Russian has two major effects:

**1. It changes pronunciation.** As you learned in the pronunciation rules lesson, unstressed vowels are reduced. If you stress the wrong syllable, every vowel in the word shifts:
- **молоко́** — stress on the last syllable → pronounced "malakó"
- If you wrongly stressed the first syllable: "móloko" — that's not how any Russian speaker says it

**2. It can change meaning.** Some words are spelled identically but have different stress and completely different meanings:

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **за́мок** *(zámok)* | castle |
| **замо́к** *(zamók)* | lock |
| **му́ка** *(múka)* | flour |
| **мука́** *(muká)* | torment, suffering |
| **бе́лки** *(bélki)* | squirrels |
| **белки́** *(belkí)* | proteins |
| **а́тлас** *(átlas)* | atlas (book of maps) |
| **атла́с** *(atlás)* | satin (fabric) |
| **о́рган** *(órgan)* | organ (body part) |
| **орга́н** *(orgán)* | organ (musical instrument) |
| **пи́ли** *(píli)* | they sawed |
| **пили́** *(pilí)* | drink! (imperative) |

**Он купил замо́к для двери за́мка.** *(On kupil zamók dlya dveri zámka.)* — He bought a lock for the castle door.

**В магазине есть му́ка, а на экзамене — мука́.** *(V magazine yest' múka, a na ekzamene — muká.)* — In the store there's flour, and at the exam — torment.

## 2. Stress Is Unpredictable

Unlike some Slavic languages, Russian stress has **no simple rule** for which syllable is stressed. It must be learned word by word. Compare these two-syllable words:

- **ма́ма** *(máma)* — mom (stress on 1st syllable)
- **вода́** *(vodá)* — water (stress on 2nd syllable)
- **сло́во** *(slóvo)* — word (stress on 1st syllable)
- **окно́** *(oknó)* — window (stress on 2nd syllable)

And these three-syllable words:

- **ко́мната** *(kómnata)* — room (stress on 1st syllable)
- **дере́вня** *(derévnya)* — village (stress on 2nd syllable)
- **молоко́** *(malakó)* — milk (stress on 3rd syllable)

There is no way to predict the stress just from looking at the spelling. Dictionaries mark stress with an accent mark (´) over the stressed vowel, and learners should always note it when learning new words.

## 3. Common Stress Patterns

While stress is unpredictable in general, there are some **tendencies** (not absolute rules) that can help:

### Feminine Nouns (ending in -а/-я)

Many two-syllable feminine nouns stress the **first syllable**:

- **ма́ма** *(máma)* — mom
- **ко́шка** *(kóshka)* — cat
- **ла́мпа** *(lámpa)* — lamp
- **ка́рта** *(kárta)* — map
- **ша́пка** *(shápka)* — hat

But many others stress the **last syllable** — so this is only a tendency:

- **жена́** *(zhená)* — wife
- **вода́** *(vodá)* — water
- **рука́** *(ruká)* — hand
- **земля́** *(zemlá)* — earth

### Masculine Nouns (ending in a consonant)

Many masculine nouns stress the **last syllable**, especially longer words:

- **студе́нт** *(studént)* — student
- **язы́к** *(yazýk)* — language
- **магази́н** *(magazín)* — store
- **телефо́н** *(telefón)* — telephone

But shorter masculine nouns often stress the **first syllable**:

- **го́род** *(górod)* — city
- **ве́чер** *(vécher)* — evening
- **у́тро** *(útro)* — morning

### Neuter Nouns (ending in -о/-е)

When the ending -о is stressed, it sounds like "o". When unstressed, it reduces to "a":

- **окно́** *(oknó)* — window (stressed ending)
- **письмо́** *(pis'mó)* — letter (stressed ending)
- **сло́во** *(slóvo)* — word (stressed stem, -о reduces)
- **мо́ре** *(móre)* — sea (stressed stem)

## 4. Stress Shifts in Declension

One of the hardest aspects of Russian stress is that it can **move to a different syllable** when a noun changes case or number. This means the stress of the dictionary form (nominative singular) doesn't always predict the stress of other forms.

### Common Shifts: Singular → Plural

- **рука́** *(ruká)* — hand → **ру́ки** *(rúki)* — hands (stress moves backward)
- **нога́** *(nogá)* — leg → **но́ги** *(nógi)* — legs
- **голова́** *(golová)* — head → **го́ловы** *(gólovy)* — heads
- **стена́** *(stená)* — wall → **сте́ны** *(sténi)* — walls

### Shifts Within Singular Cases

Some nouns shift stress even between singular cases:

- **голова́** *(golová)* — head (nominative)
- **го́лову** *(gólovu)* — head (accusative)

**Она повернула го́лову.** *(Ona povernula gólovu.)* — She turned her head.

**У неё болит голова́.** *(U neyó bolit golová.)* — She has a headache.

### Masculine Nouns: Singular → Plural Shift

Many masculine nouns shift stress to the ending in the plural:

- **го́род** *(górod)* — city → **города́** *(gorodá)* — cities
- **до́м** *(dom)* — house → **дома́** *(domá)* — houses
- **глаз** *(glaz)* — eye → **глаза́** *(glazá)* — eyes

## 5. Stress Shifts in Conjugation

Verb stress can also shift between different conjugation forms:

### First-Person Shift

Some verbs stress the ending in the first person (я) but shift to the stem in other forms:

- **писа́ть** *(pisát')* — to write:
  - **пишу́** *(pishú)* — I write (stress on ending)
  - **пи́шешь** *(píshesh')* — you write (stress on stem)
  - **пи́шет** *(píshet)* — he/she writes (stress on stem)

- **смотре́ть** *(smotrét')* — to watch:
  - **смотрю́** *(smotryú)* — I watch (stress on ending)
  - **смо́тришь** *(smótrish')* — you watch (stress on stem)

### Past Tense Shifts

Some verbs shift stress in the feminine past tense:

- **быть** *(byt')* — to be:
  - **был** *(byl)* — he was
  - **бы́ло** *(býlo)* — it was
  - **была́** *(bylá)* — she was (stress shifts to ending!)

- **брать** *(brat')* — to take:
  - **брал** *(bral)* — he took
  - **бра́ло** *(brálo)* — it took
  - **брала́** *(bralá)* — she took (stress shifts!)

**Она́ была́ до́ма.** *(Oná bylá dóma.)* — She was at home.

**Он был до́ма.** *(On byl dóma.)* — He was at home.

## 6. Tips for Learning Stress

Since stress must be memorized, here are strategies that help:

### 1. Always Learn Stress With the Word
When you learn a new word, learn its stress. Write it with the accent mark: **молоко́**, not just **молоко**.

### 2. Use a Dictionary
Russian dictionaries always mark stress. Online resources like Wiktionary show stress for every word form (all cases and conjugations).

### 3. Listen Actively
When listening to Russian, pay attention to which syllable is stressed. Stressed syllables are louder, longer, and clearer.

### 4. Learn Common Patterns
While no rule is 100% reliable, recognizing patterns helps. Words ending in **-ция** are almost always stressed on the syllable before: **ста́нция** *(stántsiya)*, **лекция** *(léktsiya)*.

### 5. Practice With Minimal Pairs
Practicing words like **за́мок/замо́к** helps train your ear to hear and produce stress differences.

**Ударение в русском языке нужно запоминать.** *(Udareniye v russkom yazyke nuzhno zapominat'.)* — Stress in Russian must be memorized.
