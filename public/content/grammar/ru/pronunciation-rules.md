# Pronunciation Rules

Russian pronunciation follows consistent rules, but they differ significantly from how words are spelled. The three most important phenomena are **vowel reduction** (unstressed vowels change sound), **consonant devoicing** (voiced consonants lose their voice in certain positions), and **palatalization** (consonants become "soft" before certain vowels). Mastering these rules will make your Russian sound natural and help you understand native speakers.

---

## 1. Vowel Reduction — The #1 Rule

In Russian, **only stressed vowels are pronounced clearly**. Unstressed vowels are "reduced" — they become shorter and shift toward different sounds. This is the single most important pronunciation rule in the language.

### Unstressed О → sounds like А

When О is not stressed, it sounds like a short "a" sound. This is called **аканье** *(akan'ye)*.

- **молоко́** *(malakó)* — milk (written О-О-О, but only the last О is stressed and sounds like "o")
- **хорошо́** *(kharashó)* — good (the first two О's sound like "a")
- **Москва́** *(Maskvá)* — Moscow (the О sounds like "a")
- **окно́** *(aknó)* — window (the first О is unstressed, so it sounds like "a")
- **собака** *(sabáka)* — dog (the first О is reduced)

This is why **молоко** is pronounced "malakó" and not "molokó" — only the stressed О keeps its true "o" sound.

### Unstressed Е → sounds like И

When Е is not stressed, it sounds closer to "i":

- **телефо́н** *(tilefón)* — telephone (the first Е sounds like "i")
- **ребёнок** *(ribyónak)* — child (the unstressed Е sounds like "i")
- **весна́** *(visná)* — spring (the Е sounds like "i")
- **земля́** *(zimlá)* — earth/land (the Е is reduced)

### Unstressed Я → sounds like И

When Я is not stressed, it also reduces toward an "i" sound:

- **язы́к** *(izýk)* — language/tongue (the Я sounds like "i")
- **яйцо́** *(yiytsó)* — egg (the unstressed Я is reduced)
- **января́** *(yinvará)* — January (genitive) (the first Я is reduced)

### Summary Table

| Letter | When Stressed | When Unstressed |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| О | "o" (as in more) | "a" (as in about) |
| Е | "ye" (as in yes) | "i" (as in it) |
| Я | "ya" (as in yard) | "i" (as in it) |
| А | "a" (as in father) | short "a" (weakened) |

## 2. Voiced & Voiceless Consonant Pairs

Russian consonants come in **voiced/voiceless pairs**. A voiced consonant uses the vocal cords; its voiceless partner is produced the same way but without vibration.

| Voiced | Voiceless | Mouth Position |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Б (b) | П (p) | lips together |
| В (v) | Ф (f) | teeth on lip |
| Г (g) | К (k) | back of tongue |
| Д (d) | Т (t) | tongue on teeth |
| Ж (zh) | Ш (sh) | tongue pulled back |
| З (z) | С (s) | tongue near teeth |

### Devoicing at the End of a Word

When a voiced consonant appears at the **end of a word**, it is pronounced as its voiceless partner. This is called **final devoicing**.

- **хлеб** *(khlep)* — bread (Б → П at the end)
- **город** *(górat)* — city (Д → Т at the end)
- **друг** *(druk)* — friend (Г → К at the end)
- **нож** *(nosh)* — knife (Ж → Ш at the end)
- **глаз** *(glas)* — eye (З → С at the end)
- **кров** *(krof)* — shelter (В → Ф at the end)

The spelling stays the same — only the pronunciation changes!

### Voicing Assimilation

When a voiceless consonant appears **before a voiced consonant**, it becomes voiced:

- **сделать** *(zdelat')* — to do (С before voiced Д → pronounced as З)
- **вокзал** *(vagzal)* — train station (К before voiced З → pronounced as Г)
- **сбор** *(zbor)* — collection (С before voiced Б → pronounced as З)

And when a voiced consonant appears **before a voiceless consonant**, it becomes voiceless:

- **ложка** *(loshka)* — spoon (Ж before voiceless К → pronounced as Ш)
- **водка** *(votka)* — vodka (Д before voiceless К → pronounced as Т)
- **завтра** *(zaftra)* — tomorrow (В before voiceless Т → pronounced as Ф)

## 3. Palatalization (Softening of Consonants)

Palatalization is when a consonant is pronounced with the middle of the tongue raised toward the hard palate, giving it a "softer" quality. In Russian, this is a critical distinction — the same consonant can be either **hard** or **soft**, and the difference can change the meaning of a word.

### How Consonants Become Soft

There are two ways a consonant becomes palatalized:

**1. Before a soft vowel (Я, Е, Ё, Ю, И):**

- **мат** *(mat)* — checkmate (hard Т) → **мять** *(myat')* — to crumple (soft М before Я)
- **лук** *(luk)* — onion (hard Л) → **люк** *(lyuk)* — hatch (soft Л before Ю)
- **нос** *(nos)* — nose (hard Н) → **нёс** *(nyos)* — carried (soft Н before Ё)
- **мыло** *(mylo)* — soap (hard М before Ы) → **мило** *(milo)* — cute (soft М before И)

**2. Before the soft sign (Ь):**

- **брат** *(brat)* — brother → **кровать** *(krovat')* — bed (soft Т)
- **угол** *(ugol)* — corner → **уголь** *(ugol')* — coal (soft Л)
- **мат** *(mat)* — checkmate → **мать** *(mat')* — mother (soft Т)
- **кон** *(kon)* — stake → **конь** *(kon')* — horse (soft Н)

### Always Hard Consonants

Three consonants are **always hard** and never become soft, even before soft vowels:

- **Ж** — always hard: **жить** *(zhit')* — to live (Ж stays hard despite И)
- **Ш** — always hard: **шить** *(shit')* — to sew (Ш stays hard despite И)
- **Ц** — always hard: **цирк** *(tsirk)* — circus (Ц stays hard despite И)

This is why **жи** and **ши** are pronounced with a hard "zh" and "sh" even though they are written with И.

### Always Soft Consonants

Two consonants are **always soft**, even without a soft vowel:

- **Ч** — always soft: **час** *(chas)* — hour (Ч is soft even before А)
- **Щ** — always soft: **щи** *(shchi)* — cabbage soup (Щ is always a long soft "sh")

## 4. Other Pronunciation Notes

### The Letter Г in Special Cases

In certain words and endings, **Г** is pronounced as **В** (v):

- **его́** *(yevó)* — his/him (Г sounds like В)
- **сего́дня** *(sevódnya)* — today (Г sounds like В)
- **ничего́** *(nichevó)* — nothing (Г sounds like В)

This happens in the genitive endings **-его** and **-ого**.

### Double Consonants

When the same consonant appears twice (or two different consonants merge), it is held slightly longer:

- **Анна** *(Anna)* — Anna (long Н)
- **русский** *(russkiy)* — Russian (long С)
- **класс** *(klass)* — class (long С)

### The Combination ЧН

In some common words, **ЧН** is pronounced as **ШН**:

- **конечно** *(kanéshna)* — of course
- **скучно** *(skúshna)* — boring
- **яичница** *(yaíshnitsa)* — fried eggs

In most other words, ЧН is pronounced as written: **точно** *(tóchna)* — exactly.

## 5. Practice Summary

The key pronunciation rules to internalize:

1. **Vowel reduction**: Unstressed О → "а", unstressed Е/Я → "и"
2. **Final devoicing**: Voiced consonants become voiceless at word end (хлеб → "хлеп")
3. **Voicing assimilation**: Consonants match the voicing of the next consonant
4. **Palatalization**: Consonants are softened before Я, Е, Ё, Ю, И and the soft sign Ь
5. **Always hard**: Ж, Ш, Ц — **Always soft**: Ч, Щ

**Чтобы хорошо говорить по-русски, нужно много практиковаться.** *(Shtoby khorosho govorit' po-russki, nuzhno mnogo praktikovat'sya.)* — To speak Russian well, you need lots of practice.
