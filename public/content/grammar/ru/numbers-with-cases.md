# Numbers with Cases (Числа с падежами)

One of the trickiest aspects of Russian grammar is how **numbers interact with nouns**. Unlike English, where you simply say "five books," Russian requires different noun forms depending on the number. The noun's case and number change based on the last digit — a system that confuses even intermediate learners but becomes second nature with practice.

---

## 1. The Basic Rule: Three Groups

Russian numbers fall into three groups based on how they affect the following noun:

| Number | Noun form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (один/одна/одно) | Nominative singular | один стол *(odín stol)* — one table |
| 2, 3, 4 | Genitive singular | два стола *(dva stolá)* — two tables |
| 5–20 | Genitive plural | пять столов *(pyat stolóv)* — five tables |

**Один дом.** *(Odín dom.)* — One house.

**Два дома.** *(Dva dóma.)* — Two houses.

**Пять домов.** *(Pyat domóv.)* — Five houses.

> This is the foundation. Everything else builds on these three groups.

---

## 2. Number 1: Full Agreement

**Один** *(odín)* agrees with its noun in **gender**, **number**, and **case** — it behaves like an adjective:

| Gender | Nominative | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Masculine | один | один студент *(odín studént)* — one student |
| Feminine | одна | одна книга *(odná kníga)* — one book |
| Neuter | одно | одно окно *(odnó oknó)* — one window |
| Plural | одни | одни часы *(odní chasý)* — one clock (pluralia tantum) |

**Одна** declines through all cases:

**У меня есть одна идея.** *(U menya yest odná idéya.)* — I have one idea. (nominative)

**Без одной минуты.** *(Bez odnóy minúty.)* — One minute to (the hour). (genitive)

**Я говорю об одном человеке.** *(Ya govoryu ob odnóm chelovéke.)* — I'm talking about one person. (prepositional)

---

## 3. Numbers 2, 3, 4: Genitive Singular

After **два/две**, **три**, and **четыре**, the noun takes the **genitive singular**:

| Number | Masculine/Neuter | Feminine | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | два | две | два брата *(dva bráta)*, две сестры *(dve sestrý)* |
| 3 | три | три | три стола *(tri stolá)* |
| 4 | четыре | четыре | четыре книги *(chetýre knígi)* |

> **Два** is used with masculine and neuter nouns; **две** is used with feminine nouns.

**Два студента пришли.** *(Dva studénta prishli.)* — Two students came.

**Три окна открыты.** *(Tri okná otkrýty.)* — Three windows are open.

**Четыре девушки ждут.** *(Chetýre dévushki zhdut.)* — Four girls are waiting.

## 4. Numbers 5–20: Genitive Plural

After numbers **5 through 20**, the noun takes the **genitive plural**:

**Пять студентов.** *(Pyat studéntov.)* — Five students.

**Шесть книг.** *(Shest knig.)* — Six books.

**Десять домов.** *(Désyat domóv.)* — Ten houses.

**Двенадцать лет.** *(Dvenádtsat let.)* — Twelve years.

**Двадцать рублей.** *(Dvádtsat rubléy.)* — Twenty rubles.

This group also includes **11, 12, 13, 14** — even though they end in 1, 2, 3, 4:

**Одиннадцать студентов** *(odínnadtsat studéntov)* — eleven students (**not** студент!)

**Двенадцать книг** *(dvenádtsat knig)* — twelve books (**not** книги!)

**Четырнадцать дней** *(chetýrnadtsat dney)* — fourteen days

> Numbers 11–14 are an exception — they always take genitive plural, ignoring the last digit rule.

---

## 5. The Last Digit Rule (for 21+)

For compound numbers (21 and above), the **last digit** determines the noun form:

| Last digit | Noun form | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (but not 11) | Nom. singular | 21 студент, 31 книга, 101 дом |
| 2, 3, 4 (but not 12-14) | Gen. singular | 22 студента, 43 книги, 104 дома |
| 5–9, 0, 11–14 | Gen. plural | 25 студентов, 30 книг, 112 домов |

**Двадцать один рубль.** *(Dvádtsat odín rubl.)* — Twenty-one rubles.

**Двадцать два рубля.** *(Dvádtsat dva rublya.)* — Twenty-two rubles.

**Двадцать пять рублей.** *(Dvádtsat pyat rubléy.)* — Twenty-five rubles.

**Тридцать одна страница.** *(Trídtsat odná straníitsa.)* — Thirty-one pages.

**Сорок три страницы.** *(Sórok tri straníitsy.)* — Forty-three pages.

**Пятьдесят страниц.** *(Pyatdesyát straníts.)* — Fifty pages.

## 6. Numbers in Oblique Cases

When the entire number-noun phrase is in an **oblique case** (genitive, dative, instrumental, prepositional), **both** the number and the noun decline:

### With 5+ (both decline, noun stays plural)

**Я говорил с пятью студентами.** *(Ya govoríl s pyatyú studéntami.)* — I spoke with five students. (instrumental)

**В шести книгах.** *(V shestí knígakh.)* — In six books. (prepositional)

**К десяти часам.** *(K desyatí chasám.)* — By ten o'clock. (dative)

### With 2, 3, 4 (both decline, noun becomes plural in oblique cases)

**С двумя братьями.** *(S dvumyá brátymi.)* — With two brothers. (instrumental)

**О трёх книгах.** *(O tryókh knígakh.)* — About three books. (prepositional)

**К четырём часам.** *(K chetyryóm chasám.)* — By four o'clock. (dative)

> In oblique cases, the noun after 2-4 takes the **plural** form (not genitive singular as in nominative/accusative).

---

## 7. Ordinal Numbers: Basics

**Ordinal numbers** (first, second, third...) behave like adjectives — they agree in gender, number, and case:

| Cardinal | Ordinal (masculine) | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|---|---|---|
| один | первый *(pérvyy)* | первая | первое |
| два | второй *(vtoróy)* | вторая | второе |
| три | третий *(trétiy)* | третья | третье |
| четыре | четвёртый *(chetvyórtyy)* | четвёртая | четвёртое |
| пять | пятый *(pyátyy)* | пятая | пятое |

**Первый урок.** *(Pérvyy urók.)* — The first lesson.

**Вторая глава.** *(Vtoráya glavá.)* — The second chapter.

**На третьем этаже.** *(Na trétyem etazhé.)* — On the third floor. (prepositional)

> In compound ordinals, only the **last** word changes: двадцать первый (twenty-first), сто третья (one hundred third).

## 8. Practical Examples: Prices, Ages, Time

### Prices

**Это стоит сто один рубль.** *(Éto stóit sto odín rubl.)* — It costs 101 rubles.

**Это стоит двести тридцать четыре рубля.** *(Éto stóit dvésti trídtsat chetýre rublya.)* — It costs 234 rubles.

**Это стоит пятьсот рублей.** *(Éto stóit pyatsót rubléy.)* — It costs 500 rubles.

### Ages

**Мне двадцать один год.** *(Mne dvádtsat odín god.)* — I'm 21 years old.

**Ему тридцать два года.** *(Yemú trídtsat dva góda.)* — He's 32 years old.

**Ей сорок пять лет.** *(Yey sórok pyat let.)* — She's 45 years old.

> The word for "year" changes: год (1), года (2-4), лет (5-20).

### Time

**Сейчас три часа.** *(Seychás tri chasá.)* — It's three o'clock.

**Встреча в пять часов.** *(Vstrécha v pyat chasóv.)* — The meeting is at five o'clock.

## 9. Common Mistakes

❌ **Двадцать один рублей.** — Last digit is 1, so use nominative singular.
✅ **Двадцать один рубль.** — Twenty-one rubles.

❌ **Два сестры.** — Два is for masculine/neuter; use две for feminine.
✅ **Две сестры.** — Two sisters.

❌ **Одиннадцать студент.** — 11 takes genitive plural, not singular.
✅ **Одиннадцать студентов.** — Eleven students.

❌ **Пять книги.** — After 5+, use genitive plural.
✅ **Пять книг.** — Five books.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **1** — noun in nominative singular; один agrees in gender.
2. **2, 3, 4** — noun in genitive singular; два/две distinguishes gender.
3. **5–20** — noun in genitive plural.
4. **21+** — follow the last digit rule (except 11–14, which always take genitive plural).
5. In oblique cases, both the number and noun decline.
6. Ordinal numbers act like adjectives and agree in gender, number, and case.
