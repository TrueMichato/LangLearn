# Giving & Receiving Actions (てあげる、てもらう、てくれる)

Japanese has a unique system for describing when someone does a favor for another person. Unlike English, the verb changes depending on **who gives the favor**, **who receives it**, and **the social relationship** between them. This is one of the most culturally important grammar points in Japanese.

## Review: The Three Giving/Receiving Verbs

Before diving into the action forms, recall the basic giving/receiving verbs:

| Verb | Direction | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| あげる | I/we → others | Give (to someone) |
| くれる | Others → I/we | Give (to me/my in-group) |
| もらう | I/we ← others | Receive (from someone) |

These verbs attach to the **て-form** to describe doing favors — actions done for someone's benefit.

## てあげる — "Do something for someone"

**てあげる** means the speaker (or a third party) does a favor **for someone else**. The favor flows **away from** the speaker.

**Formation:** て-form + あげる

### Examples

> 友達(ともだち)に日本語(にほんご)を教(おし)えてあげた。
> *Tomodachi ni Nihongo wo oshiete ageta.*
> I taught Japanese to my friend (as a favor).

> 母(はは)に花(はな)を買(か)ってあげました。
> *Haha ni hana wo katte agemashita.*
> I bought flowers for my mother.

> 彼女(かのじょ)の荷物(にもつ)を持(も)ってあげた。
> *Kanojo no nimotsu wo motte ageta.*
> I carried her luggage for her.

> 道(みち)に迷(まよ)っている人(ひと)に場所(ばしょ)を教(おし)えてあげた。
> *Michi ni mayotte iru hito ni basho wo oshiete ageta.*
> I told a lost person where to go.

> 弟(おとうと)に自転車(じてんしゃ)の乗(の)り方(かた)を教(おし)えてあげよう。
> *Otouto ni jitensha no norikata wo oshiete ageyou.*
> I'll teach my little brother how to ride a bicycle.

> 💡 Be careful using **てあげる** directly to someone — it can sound condescending, as if you're highlighting your own generosity. It's safer to use it when talking **about** a favor, not **to** the person you helped.

## てくれる — "Someone does something for me/us"

**てくれる** means someone else does a favor **for the speaker** (or the speaker's in-group). The favor flows **toward** the speaker.

**Formation:** て-form + くれる

### Examples

> 友達(ともだち)が宿題(しゅくだい)を手伝(てつだ)ってくれた。
> *Tomodachi ga shukudai wo tetsudatte kureta.*
> My friend helped me with my homework.

> 母(はは)が弁当(べんとう)を作(つく)ってくれました。
> *Haha ga bentou wo tsukutte kuremashita.*
> My mother made me a bento.

> 先生(せんせい)が分(わ)かりやすく説明(せつめい)してくれた。
> *Sensei ga wakariyasuku setsumei shite kureta.*
> The teacher explained it to me in an easy-to-understand way.

> 彼(かれ)が駅(えき)まで送(おく)ってくれた。
> *Kare ga eki made okutte kureta.*
> He gave me a ride to the station.

> 誰(だれ)も教(おし)えてくれなかった。
> *Dare mo oshiete kurenakatta.*
> Nobody told me. (Nobody did me the favor of telling me.)

> 💡 **てくれる** expresses gratitude — by using it, you acknowledge the action as a kindness done for you.

## てもらう — "Receive the favor of someone doing something"

**てもらう** means the speaker **receives a favor** — someone does something at the speaker's request or for their benefit.

**Formation:** て-form + もらう

The person who does the action is marked with **に**:

### Examples

> 友達(ともだち)に引(ひ)っ越(こ)しを手伝(てつだ)ってもらった。
> *Tomodachi ni hikkoshi wo tetsudatte moratta.*
> I had my friend help me move.

> 先生(せんせい)に作文(さくぶん)を直(なお)してもらいました。
> *Sensei ni sakubun wo naoshite moraimashita.*
> I had the teacher correct my essay.

> 彼(かれ)に写真(しゃしん)を撮(と)ってもらった。
> *Kare ni shashin wo totte moratta.*
> I had him take a photo (for me).

> 美容師(びようし)に髪(かみ)を切(き)ってもらった。
> *Biyoushi ni kami wo kitte moratta.*
> I had the hairdresser cut my hair.

> 日本人(にほんじん)の友達(ともだち)に発音(はつおん)をチェックしてもらっている。
> *Nihonjin no tomodachi ni hatsuon wo chekku shite moratte iru.*
> I'm having my Japanese friend check my pronunciation.

> 💡 **てもらう** implies the speaker **initiated or benefited** from the action. It's the most natural way to describe services you receive.

## Direction of Favor — Summary Table

| Pattern | Direction | Subject | Beneficiary |
|---|---|---|---|
| てあげる | Speaker → Others | Speaker / 3rd party | Someone else |
| てくれる | Others → Speaker | Someone else | Speaker / in-group |
| てもらう | Others → Speaker | Speaker (receives) | Speaker / in-group |

### Visual Flow

| Situation | Japanese | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| I help my friend | 友達(ともだち)を手伝(てつだ)って**あげる** | I → Friend |
| My friend helps me | 友達(ともだち)が手伝(てつだ)って**くれる** | Friend → Me |
| I get my friend to help | 友達(ともだち)に手伝(てつだ)って**もらう** | Friend → Me (I initiated) |

### てくれる vs てもらう — What's the difference?

Both describe receiving a favor, but:

| てくれる | てもらう |
|---|---|
| Focus on the **giver** | Focus on the **receiver** |
| The other person kindly did it | I received the favor / arranged it |
| Subject = the person who helped | Subject = the person who was helped |
| Spontaneous kindness | Often involves a request |

> 田中(たなか)さんが手伝(てつだ)ってくれた。— Tanaka helped me. (He was kind enough to help.)
> 田中(たなか)さんに手伝(てつだ)ってもらった。— I got Tanaka to help me. (I received the favor.)

## Honorific Versions

In formal situations, use the honorific equivalents:

| Casual | Honorific | Level |
|---|---|---|
| てあげる | て**さしあげる** | Humble (I do for someone superior) |
| てくれる | て**くださる** | Respectful (superior does for me) |
| てもらう | て**いただく** | Humble (I receive from someone superior) |

### てさしあげる — Humble: Doing for a superior

> 社長(しゃちょう)に資料(しりょう)を送(おく)ってさしあげました。
> *Shachou ni shiryou wo okutte sashiagemashita.*
> I sent the materials to the president (humbly, as a favor).

> お客様(きゃくさま)のお荷物(にもつ)をお持(も)ちしてさしあげます。
> *Okyakusama no onimotsu wo omochi shite sashiagemasu.*
> I will carry the guest's luggage (for them).

> 💡 **てさしあげる** can sound overly self-congratulatory. In modern business Japanese, many people avoid it and use **お〜する** patterns instead.

### てくださる — Respectful: A superior does for me

> 先生(せんせい)が推薦状(すいせんじょう)を書(か)いてくださいました。
> *Sensei ga suisenjou wo kaite kudasaimashita.*
> The teacher kindly wrote a recommendation letter for me.

> 部長(ぶちょう)がアドバイスをしてくださった。
> *Buchou ga adobaisu wo shite kudasatta.*
> The department head kindly gave me advice.

> 先輩(せんぱい)が手伝(てつだ)ってくださいました。
> *Senpai ga tetsudatte kudasaimashita.*
> My senior kindly helped me.

### ていただく — Humble: Receiving a favor from a superior

> 先生(せんせい)に推薦状(すいせんじょう)を書(か)いていただきました。
> *Sensei ni suisenjou wo kaite itadakimashita.*
> I had the teacher write a recommendation letter (humbly received the favor).

> 社長(しゃちょう)に話(はなし)を聞(き)いていただけますか？
> *Shachou ni hanashi wo kiite itadakemasu ka?*
> Could I have the president hear what I have to say?

> お名前(なまえ)を教(おし)えていただけませんか？
> *Onamae wo oshiete itadakemasen ka?*
> Could you please tell me your name? (very polite)

> 💡 **〜ていただけませんか** is one of the most polite ways to make a request in Japanese. It literally means "Could I receive the favor of you doing ~?"

## Social Dynamics: When to Use Each

### Casual situations (friends, family)

| Situation | Expression |
|---|---|
| You helped a friend | 〜てあげた |
| A friend helped you | 〜てくれた |
| You got a friend to help | 〜てもらった |

### Formal situations (boss, teacher, customer)

| Situation | Expression |
|---|---|
| You did something for a superior | 〜てさしあげた (or お〜した) |
| A superior did something for you | 〜てくださった |
| You received a favor from a superior | 〜ていただいた |

### Making requests (polite → most polite)

| Expression | Politeness |
|---|---|
| 〜てくれる？ | Casual |
| 〜てくれませんか？ | Polite |
| 〜てもらえますか？ | Polite |
| 〜ていただけますか？ | Very polite |
| 〜ていただけませんか？ | Most polite |

## Common Mistakes

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| 私は友達に教えてくれた | 友達が私に教えてくれた | くれる: the giver is the subject |
| 先生が教えてあげた (about yourself) | 先生に教えてもらった | Use もらう when you receive |
| 手伝ってあげてください | 手伝ってくれませんか | Don't combine あげる with requests to others |

## Summary

| Pattern | Meaning | Honorific |
|---|---|---|
| てあげる | Do for someone (favor outward) | てさしあげる |
| てくれる | Someone does for me (favor inward) | てくださる |
| てもらう | I receive the favor of ~ | ていただく |

Mastering these patterns is essential for sounding natural in Japanese. They reflect the deep cultural emphasis on **gratitude**, **social hierarchy**, and **awareness of relationships** that permeates the language.
