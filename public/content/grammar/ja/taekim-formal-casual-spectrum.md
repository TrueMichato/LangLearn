# Speech Level Overview (Casual to Formal)

Japanese has a rich system of speech levels that reflect the relationship between speaker and listener. This is not just about being "polite" — choosing the right level is a core part of communication. This lesson maps out the full spectrum.

## Level 1: Plain / Casual Form

Plain form uses dictionary endings (だ, る, い) with no ます or です. Used with close friends, family, and in your own thoughts.

- **これ、おいしい。** (Kore, oishii.) — This is good.
- **明日行く。** (Ashita iku.) — I'm going tomorrow.
- **もう食べた？** (Mou tabeta?) — Did you already eat?
- **あの映画、面白かった。** (Ano eiga, omoshirokatta.) — That movie was interesting.

Plain form is also used inside subordinate clauses even in polite speech: 明日行く**と思います** (I think I'll go tomorrow).

## Level 2: Polite Form (です/ます)

The standard polite form. Used with strangers, acquaintances, coworkers, and in most public situations. This is the "default" level for adult conversation.

- **これはおいしいです。** (Kore wa oishii desu.) — This is delicious.
- **明日行きます。** (Ashita ikimasu.) — I will go tomorrow.
- **もう食べましたか。** (Mou tabemashita ka.) — Have you already eaten?

> 💡 Most textbooks teach polite form first, but plain form is what you hear in anime, manga, and casual conversation. Both are essential.

## Level 3: Honorific Language (尊敬語)

Honorific language (尊敬語 / sonkeigo) **elevates the actions of others** to show respect. You never use it for your own actions.

### お/ご Prefix

Adding お or ご to nouns and some verbs adds respect: お名前 (your name), ご家族 (your family), お忙しい (you are busy).

### Special Honorific Verbs

Many common verbs have dedicated honorific replacements:

| Plain | Honorific | Meaning |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| 行く/来る | いらっしゃる | go / come / be |
| 言う | おっしゃる | say |
| 食べる/飲む | 召し上がる | eat / drink |
| 見る | ご覧になる | look / see |
| する | なさる | do |
| くれる | くださる | give (to me) |
| 知っている | ご存じだ | know |

- **先生はもういらっしゃいますか。** (Sensei wa mou irasshaimasu ka.) — Has the teacher arrived?
- **社長がおっしゃったことは正しいです。** (Shachou ga osshatta koto wa tadashii desu.) — What the company president said is correct.
- **どうぞ召し上がってください。** (Douzo meshiagatte kudasai.) — Please eat. (to a guest)

## Level 4: Humble Language (謙譲語)

Humble language (謙譲語 / kenjougo) **lowers your own actions** to show respect toward the listener or a third party. It is the mirror of honorific language.

### Special Humble Verbs

| Plain | Humble | Meaning |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 行く/来る | 参る | go / come |
| 言う | 申す | say |
| 食べる/飲む | いただく | eat / drink |
| する | いたす | do |
| 見る | 拝見する | look / see |
| もらう | いただく | receive |
| 知っている | 存じている | know |

- **私が参ります。** (Watashi ga mairimasu.) — I will go/come. (humble)
- **田中と申します。** (Tanaka to moushimasu.) — My name is Tanaka. (humble self-introduction)
- **メールを拝見しました。** (Meeru wo haiken shimashita.) — I have read your email. (humble)
- **少々お待ちいたします。** (Shoushou omachi itashimasu.) — I will wait a moment. (humble)

> 💡 Honorific = raise others up. Humble = lower yourself. Together they create respectful distance.

## Level 5: Ultra-Polite (ございます)

ございます is the most polite form of ある (to exist/be). It appears in fixed expressions and very formal service language.

- **ありがとうございます。** (Arigatou gozaimasu.) — Thank you very much.
- **おはようございます。** (Ohayou gozaimasu.) — Good morning. (polite)
- **お手洗いはあちらでございます。** (Otearai wa achira de gozaimasu.) — The restroom is over there. (department store level)

## Practical Tips

**Mixing is normal.** In real conversation, people often mix levels within a single exchange. You might use です/ます with someone and occasionally drop into casual form as you get comfortable.

**Match your partner.** If the other person uses casual form, you can gradually shift to casual too. If they use です/ます, stick with it unless they signal otherwise.

**When in doubt, be polite.** Using です/ます with someone who expects casual form is slightly awkward but harmless. Using casual form with someone who expects politeness can be genuinely rude.

**Written vs spoken.** Formal written Japanese (news, academic) often uses plain form — not because it is casual, but because it is the impersonal, objective style.

## Summary

| Level | Style | When to use |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| Plain (だ/る) | Casual | Friends, family, inner thoughts |
| Polite (です/ます) | Standard | Strangers, work, daily life |
| Honorific (尊敬語) | Elevate others | About superiors/clients |
| Humble (謙譲語) | Lower yourself | About your own actions to superiors |
| ございます | Ultra-polite | Service, ceremonies |

Mastering speech levels takes time and exposure. Start with polite form as your base, learn to read casual form for comprehension, and gradually add honorific and humble forms as you encounter them.
