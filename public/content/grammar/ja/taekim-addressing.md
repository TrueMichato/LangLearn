# Names, Titles & Pronouns

Japanese handles personal reference very differently from English. Rather than relying heavily on pronouns like "I" and "you," Japanese speakers prefer to use **names** and **titles**, or drop the subject entirely when context makes it clear. Understanding this system is key to sounding natural.

## Why Japanese Avoids Pronouns

In English, we constantly say "I did this" and "you said that." In Japanese, the subject is usually **omitted** when it's obvious from context:

- **もう食べた？** (Mou tabeta?) — Already ate? *(= Did you eat already?)*
- **うん、食べた。** (Un, tabeta.) — Yeah, ate. *(= Yeah, I ate.)*

Neither sentence contains a word for "I" or "you" — the context makes it clear. Overusing pronouns, especially あなた, can sound unnatural or even rude.

## Honorific Suffixes

Instead of pronouns, Japanese attaches **suffixes** to names to show respect and social awareness.

### さん (san) — Neutral Respect
The most common suffix. Used for anyone you don't know well, regardless of gender or age.

- **田中さん** (Tanaka-san) — Mr./Ms. Tanaka
- **由美さん** (Yumi-san) — Yumi (polite)

You never use さん for **yourself** — it would sound like bragging.

### くん (kun) — Familiar / Junior Male
Used for boys, younger males, or male colleagues of equal or lower status. Some workplaces use it for women too.

- **太郎くん** (Tarou-kun) — Taro (friendly)

### ちゃん (chan) — Cute / Affectionate
Used for children, close female friends, or anyone you feel close to. It carries a warm, cute tone.

- **花ちゃん** (Hana-chan) — Hana (affectionate)
- **お母ちゃん** (Okaachan) — Mommy

### さま (sama) — High Respect
Very formal. Used for customers (お客様), in letters, or when addressing someone of much higher status.

- **お客様** (Okyaku-sama) — Valued customer
- **田中様** (Tanaka-sama) — Mr./Ms. Tanaka (very formal)

### 先生 (sensei) — Teacher / Expert
Used for teachers, doctors, lawyers, and other respected professionals. It replaces さん entirely:

- **山田先生** (Yamada-sensei) — Professor/Dr. Yamada

## First Name vs Last Name

In Japanese, **last names** are used by default. Using someone's first name implies closeness. The progression typically goes:

1. 田中さん (last name + さん) — standard, polite
2. 由美さん (first name + さん) — friendly but respectful
3. 由美ちゃん / 由美 (first name + ちゃん or no suffix) — close friends

Switching from last name to first name is a social milestone — it often happens when both parties agree to it.

## First-Person Pronouns (I / Me)

Japanese has several words for "I," and your choice signals your gender, age, and the formality of the situation:

| Pronoun | Reading | Usage |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 私 | わたし (watashi) | Standard, polite, gender-neutral |
| 私 | わたくし (watakushi) | Very formal |
| 僕 | ぼく (boku) | Casual, typically male |
| 俺 | おれ (ore) | Very casual, masculine, rough |
| あたし | atashi | Casual, feminine |

- **私は学生です。** (Watashi wa gakusei desu.) — I am a student. *(neutral/polite)*
- **僕は田中です。** (Boku wa Tanaka desu.) — I'm Tanaka. *(casual male)*

**Beginner tip:** Start with **私 (watashi)** — it works in all situations for all genders.

## Referring to Others

### あなた (anata) — Use Sparingly!
While textbooks teach あなた as "you," it is rarely used in real conversation. It can sound **condescending or overly intimate** (wives sometimes call their husbands あなた). Instead, use the person's **name + suffix**:

- ❌ あなたは何を食べますか？
- ✅ 田中さんは何を食べますか？ (Tanaka-san wa nani o tabemasu ka?)

### 彼 / 彼女 (kare / kanojo) — He / She
These pronouns also double as "boyfriend" and "girlfriend," so context matters:

- **彼は学生です。** (Kare wa gakusei desu.) — He is a student. / My boyfriend is a student.
- **彼女は先生です。** (Kanojo wa sensei desu.) — She is a teacher. / My girlfriend is a teacher.

In practice, Japanese speakers prefer using the person's name over 彼/彼女.

## Summary

| Concept | Key Point |
|---------|-----------|
| Default reference | Use names, not pronouns |
| Subject omission | Drop the subject when context is clear |
| さん / くん / ちゃん / さま | Choose based on relationship and formality |
| 先生 | For teachers, doctors, experts |
| 私 (watashi) | Safest first-person pronoun |
| あなた | Avoid — use names instead |

Mastering how to address people is a social skill as much as a grammar skill. When in doubt, use the person's last name + さん — you can never go wrong with that!
