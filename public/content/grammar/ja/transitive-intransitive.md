# Transitive & Intransitive Verbs (開ける/開く)

Japanese has many verb pairs where one is **transitive** (takes a direct object — someone does it) and the other is **intransitive** (no direct object — it happens on its own). Understanding these pairs is essential for natural-sounding Japanese.

## What Are Transitive & Intransitive Verbs?

**Transitive (他動詞 / たどうし):** The subject performs an action on an object. Uses the particle **を**.

> 私(わたし)はドアを**開(あ)けた**。
> *Watashi wa doa wo **aketa**.*
> I **opened** the door.

**Intransitive (自動詞 / じどうし):** The subject undergoes the action or it happens naturally. Uses the particle **が**.

> ドアが**開(あ)いた**。
> *Doa ga **aita**.*
> The door **opened**.

Notice: In the transitive sentence, "I" acts on "the door." In the intransitive sentence, "the door" is the subject and opens by itself.

## Common Transitive/Intransitive Pairs

| Transitive (〜を) | Meaning | Intransitive (〜が) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 開(あ)ける | to open (something) | 開(あ)く | to open (by itself) |
| 閉(し)める | to close (something) | 閉(し)まる | to close (by itself) |
| 付(つ)ける | to turn on | 付(つ)く | to turn on (by itself) |
| 消(け)す | to turn off / erase | 消(き)える | to go out / disappear |
| 入(い)れる | to put in | 入(はい)る | to enter |
| 出(だ)す | to take out | 出(で)る | to come out |
| 壊(こわ)す | to break (something) | 壊(こわ)れる | to break (by itself) |
| 落(お)とす | to drop (something) | 落(お)ちる | to fall |
| 始(はじ)める | to start (something) | 始(はじ)まる | to start (by itself) |
| 終(お)わらせる | to end (something) | 終(お)わる | to end (by itself) |
| 変(か)える | to change (something) | 変(か)わる | to change (by itself) |
| 集(あつ)める | to gather (things) | 集(あつ)まる | to gather (people come together) |

## Recognizing Patterns

While there's no single rule, there are some common patterns:

### Pattern 1: 〜える (transitive) / 〜ある (intransitive)

| Transitive | Intransitive |
|---|---|
| 閉(し)める | 閉(し)まる |
| 始(はじ)める | 始(はじ)まる |
| 上(あ)げる | 上(あ)がる |
| 下(さ)げる | 下(さ)がる |
| 集(あつ)める | 集(あつ)まる |

### Pattern 2: 〜す (transitive) / 〜える or 〜る (intransitive)

| Transitive | Intransitive |
|---|---|
| 消(け)す | 消(き)える |
| 出(だ)す | 出(で)る |
| 壊(こわ)す | 壊(こわ)れる |
| 直(なお)す | 直(なお)る |

### Pattern 3: 〜ける (transitive) / 〜く (intransitive)

| Transitive | Intransitive |
|---|---|
| 開(あ)ける | 開(あ)く |
| 付(つ)ける | 付(つ)く |
| 届(とど)ける | 届(とど)く |

## Particles: を vs が

The key grammatical difference:

**Transitive verbs** → the object takes **を**:
> 先生(せんせい)が授業(じゅぎょう)を**始(はじ)めた**。
> *Sensei ga jugyou wo **hajimeta**.*
> The teacher **started** the class.

**Intransitive verbs** → the subject takes **が**:
> 授業(じゅぎょう)が**始(はじ)まった**。
> *Jugyou ga **hajimatta**.*
> The class **started**.

> 💡 A common mistake is using を with intransitive verbs. You cannot say ×「ドアを開いた」. The correct form is 「ドアが開いた」 or 「ドアを開けた」.

## Describing States with ている

Both types pair with **ている** but with different meanings:

### Transitive + ている → ongoing action or resultant state

> 母(はは)は窓(まど)を開(あ)けている。
> *Haha wa mado wo akete iru.*
> My mother is opening the window. / My mother has the window open.

### Intransitive + ている → resultant state

> 窓(まど)が開(あ)いている。
> *Mado ga aite iru.*
> The window is open (it's in the state of having opened).

This distinction matters in daily life. When describing the **current state** of something (the door is open, the light is on), Japanese overwhelmingly uses the **intransitive + ている** form:

| Japanese | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 電気(でんき)が付(つ)いている | The light is on |
| 店(みせ)が閉(し)まっている | The store is closed |
| 花瓶(かびん)が壊(こわ)れている | The vase is broken |
| 財布(さいふ)が落(お)ちている | A wallet has fallen / is lying there |

## Choosing the Right Verb

Use **transitive** when:
- You want to emphasize **who** did the action
- Someone deliberately does something

> 田中(たなか)さんがエアコンを消(け)した。
> *Tanaka-san ga eakon wo keshita.*
> Tanaka turned off the air conditioner.

Use **intransitive** when:
- The action happens **naturally** or the cause is unknown
- You want to describe a **state**

> エアコンが消(き)えた。
> *Eakon ga kieta.*
> The air conditioner turned off (by itself).

> 💡 Japanese tends to prefer intransitive expressions when the agent is unimportant. Instead of saying "Someone broke the vase," Japanese speakers are more likely to say 花瓶が壊れた (The vase broke).

## Common Mistakes

❌ コップを落ちた。 (Using を with intransitive 落ちる)
✅ コップが落ちた。 (The cup fell.)
✅ コップを落とした。 (I dropped the cup.)

❌ ドアを閉まった。 (Using を with intransitive 閉まる)
✅ ドアが閉まった。 (The door closed.)
✅ ドアを閉めた。 (I closed the door.)

## Practice Pairs

Try to produce both sentences for each situation:

1. **The light** → 電気(でんき)を付(つ)けた (I turned it on) / 電気が付(つ)いた (It turned on)
2. **The class** → 授業(じゅぎょう)を始(はじ)めた (I started it) / 授業が始(はじ)まった (It started)
3. **My bag** → かばんを落(お)とした (I dropped it) / かばんが落(お)ちた (It fell)
4. **The shape** → 形(かたち)を変(か)えた (I changed it) / 形が変(か)わった (It changed)

## Summary

| Aspect | Transitive (他動詞) | Intransitive (自動詞) |
|---|---|---|
| Particle | を | が |
| Agent | Someone does it | It happens naturally |
| ている | Action in progress / state | Resultant state |
| Focus | Who did it | What happened |

Mastering transitive and intransitive pairs is one of the keys to sounding natural in Japanese. Pay attention to which verb native speakers choose — it reveals how they think about events!
