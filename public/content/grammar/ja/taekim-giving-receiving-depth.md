# Giving & Receiving Actions (あげる・くれる・もらう)

Japanese has three verbs for giving and receiving, each encoding the **direction** of the action relative to the speaker. This system extends to doing favors with て-form, making it one of the most expressive and culturally important patterns in the language.

## The Three Core Verbs

| Verb | Direction | Perspective |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| あげる | I/someone → others (away) | Giver's perspective |
| くれる | Someone → me/my group (toward) | Receiver's perspective |
| もらう | I/my group ← someone (receive) | Receiver's perspective |

- **私は友達にプレゼントをあげた。** (Watashi wa tomodachi ni purezento wo ageta.) — I gave my friend a present.
- **友達が私にプレゼントをくれた。** (Tomodachi ga watashi ni purezento wo kureta.) — My friend gave me a present.
- **私は友達にプレゼントをもらった。** (Watashi wa tomodachi ni purezento wo moratta.) — I received a present from my friend.

> 💡 あげる and くれる both mean "to give," but くれる always means the giving **benefits me or my in-group**.

## The Key Distinction: あげる vs くれる

This is where many learners stumble. Both mean "give," but the choice depends on who benefits.

**あげる** — giving away from the speaker's circle:
- **弟にお菓子をあげた。** (Otouto ni okashi wo ageta.) — I gave my little brother sweets.
- **田中さんは山田さんに花をあげた。** (Tanaka-san wa Yamada-san ni hana wo ageta.) — Tanaka gave Yamada flowers. (neutral, neither is my in-group)

**くれる** — giving toward the speaker's circle:
- **先生が本をくれた。** (Sensei ga hon wo kureta.) — The teacher gave me a book.
- **友達が妹にお土産をくれた。** (Tomodachi ga imouto ni omiyage wo kureta.) — My friend gave my sister a souvenir. (sister = my in-group)

Using くれる implies gratitude. Choosing あげる when くれる is appropriate sounds cold or detached.

## て-Form: Doing Favors

The real power of this system emerges when combined with て-form verbs. This expresses doing something **as a favor**.

**てあげる** — I do something for someone else:
- **友達に日本語を教えてあげた。** (Tomodachi ni nihongo wo oshiete ageta.) — I taught my friend Japanese (as a favor).
- **荷物を持ってあげましょうか。** (Nimotsu wo motte agemashou ka.) — Shall I carry your bags for you?

**てくれる** — Someone does something for me (with gratitude):
- **母が弁当を作ってくれた。** (Haha ga bentou wo tsukutte kureta.) — My mom made me a bento (and I appreciate it).
- **友達が駅まで送ってくれた。** (Tomodachi ga eki made okutte kureta.) — My friend took me to the station (I'm grateful).

**てもらう** — I receive the favor of someone doing something:
- **先生に推薦状を書いてもらった。** (Sensei ni suisenjou wo kaite moratta.) — I had the teacher write me a recommendation letter.
- **友達に手伝ってもらった。** (Tomodachi ni tetsudatte moratta.) — I had my friend help me (I received the favor).

## Common Mistake: あげる vs くれる

- ❌ ~~友達が教えてあげた~~ (when talking about a favor TO you)
- ✅ **友達が教えてくれた。** — My friend taught me. (gratitude implied)

If someone does something that benefits you, always use くれる. Using あげる in this case sounds like you are describing the event from the other person's viewpoint, which is unnatural.

## The Politeness Upgrade

Each verb has a polite/honorific counterpart:

| Plain | Polite/Honorific | Usage |
|-------|-----------------|-------|
| あげる | さしあげる | Giving to a social superior |
| くれる | くださる | A superior gives to me |
| もらう | いただく | I humbly receive from a superior |

- **先生が教えてくださった。** (Sensei ga oshiete kudasatta.) — The teacher kindly taught me. (honorific)
- **部長にサインしていただいた。** (Buchou ni sain shite itadaita.) — I had the department head sign it. (humble)
- **お客様にお茶をさしあげてください。** (Okyakusama ni ocha wo sashiagete kudasai.) — Please serve tea to the customer. (humble giving)

## Summary

| Pattern | Meaning | Nuance |
|---------|---------|--------|
| てあげる | Do for someone | Favor going out |
| てくれる | Someone does for me | Gratitude |
| てもらう | I receive the favor | I'm the beneficiary |

This system reflects how deeply Japanese embeds social awareness into grammar. Every act of giving or helping encodes who benefits and how grateful you feel.
