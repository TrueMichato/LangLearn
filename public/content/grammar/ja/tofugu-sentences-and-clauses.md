# Building Sentences and Clauses

> **Source:** Content from [Tofugu](https://www.tofugu.com/japanese-grammar/sentences-and-clauses/). For personal study.

Understanding clauses is key to breaking down and constructing Japanese sentences. Japanese is an SOV (Subject-Object-Verb) language, and the only truly essential clause element is the **predicate**.

## Simple Sentences

A simple sentence contains one clause. Every clause has a "thing" and "info about that thing":

| Thing | Info About Thing |
|---|---|
| (バスが) | 来る |
| (空が) | 明るい |
| (これが) | 私の家です |

In Japanese, the "thing" (subject) is often omitted when clear from context.

## The Predicate

The predicate is the only **essential** element of a Japanese clause. It can be:

- **Noun:** (それは)犬。 — That is a dog.
- **な-adjective:** (花子は)きれい。 — Hanako is pretty.
- **い-adjective:** (今日は)暑い。 — Today is hot.
- **Verb:** (今から)歌う。 — Now I will sing.

## Nonessential Clause Elements

These dress up the predicate with additional information:

### Object (を)
- **お寿司を**作る。 — Make sushi.

### Subject (が)
- **お父さんが**お寿司を作る。 — My father makes sushi.

### Topic (は)
- **毎週日曜日は**お父さんがお寿司を作る。 — Every Sunday, my father makes sushi.

### Adverbials (に, で, etc.)
- 毎週日曜日はお父さんが**台所で**お寿司を作る。 — Every Sunday, my father makes sushi **in the kitchen**.

### Sentence Final Particles
- 毎週日曜日はお父さんが台所でお寿司を作る**の**。 — (adds explanatory nuance)

## Sentence Order

Japanese is SOV: Subject → Object → Verb

- [お父さんが] [お寿司を] [作る] — My father makes sushi.

However, thanks to particles, word order is flexible in Japanese. You can rearrange for emphasis.

## Complex Sentences

### Linked Clauses
Clauses can be connected using conjunctive particles or conjugation:

**With particle から:**
- 今日は忙しい**から**、ストレスが溜まっている。 — I'm busy today, **so** I'm stressed.

**With て form:**
- 今日は忙しく**て**、ストレスが溜まっている。 — I'm busy today, **and** I'm stressed.

### Embedded Clauses

**Quotation (と):**
- 弟は「火星に住みたい」**と**言いました。 — My brother said he wants to live on Mars.

**Noun Modification:**
- **スーパーの前で拾った**猫 — the cat **that I found in front of the supermarket**

In Japanese, noun modifiers always come **before** the noun they modify.

## です and ます in Clauses

- In **linked clauses**, both clauses can have です/ます, but a single marker at the end suffices:
  - 桜が満開**だ**から、お花見に行き**ます**。
- In **embedded clauses**, です/ます only appears in direct quotes, not indirect:
  - Direct: 「桜が満開**です**よ」と教えてもらいました。
  - Indirect: 桜が満開**だ**と教えてもらった。
