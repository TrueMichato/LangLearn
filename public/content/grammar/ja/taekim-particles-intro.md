# Core Particle Foundations — は, も, が

Particles are one or two hiragana characters that attach after words to define their grammatical role. They are the glue of Japanese sentences. In this lesson, we focus on three of the most important particles: the topic marker **は**, the inclusive **も**, and the subject identifier **が**.

## は (wa) — The Topic Marker

The particle **は** (pronounced "wa," not "ha") marks what you are talking **about**. Think of it as saying: "As for X, ..." Everything that follows describes or comments on that topic.

- **田中さんは学生だ。** (Tanaka-san wa gakusei da.) — As for Tanaka, (he) is a student.
- **今日は暑い。** (Kyō wa atsui.) — As for today, (it) is hot.
- **朝ご飯はパンだ。** (Asagohan wa pan da.) — As for breakfast, (it) is bread.

The topic does not have to be the grammatical subject. It simply sets the frame for the conversation:

- **映画は昨日見た。** (Eiga wa kinō mita.) — As for the movie, (I) watched (it) yesterday.

Here, the movie is the topic, but "I" am the one who watched it.

## も (mo) — "Also" / "Too"

The particle **も** replaces は (or が/を) to express that something **also** applies. It works like "too" or "also" in English.

- **田中さんは学生だ。** (Tanaka-san wa gakusei da.) — Tanaka is a student.
- **山田さんも学生だ。** (Yamada-san mo gakusei da.) — Yamada is **also** a student.

Notice that も takes the place of は — you do not say は + も together.

- **これも美味しい。** (Kore mo oishii.) — This is **also** delicious.
- **私もわからない。** (Watashi mo wakaranai.) — I **also** don't understand.

You can chain も to list multiple items that share a property:

- **お茶も水も好きだ。** (Ocha mo mizu mo suki da.) — (I) like both tea and water.

## が (ga) — The Subject Identifier

The particle **が** identifies the **subject** — specifically who or what does the action or has the attribute. It often answers a "who?" or "what?" question and introduces new or emphasized information.

- **誰が来た？** (Dare ga kita?) — **Who** came?
- **田中さんが来た。** (Tanaka-san ga kita.) — **Tanaka** came. (It's Tanaka who came.)
- **雨が降っている。** (Ame ga futte iru.) — Rain is falling.

が is also required with certain constructions, such as expressing likes, abilities, and existence:

- **猫が好きだ。** (Neko ga suki da.) — (I) like cats. (Literally: Cats are liked.)
- **日本語がわかる。** (Nihongo ga wakaru.) — (I) understand Japanese.

## The Crucial は vs が Distinction

This is one of the trickiest parts of Japanese. Here is a simplified way to think about it:

| は (topic) | が (subject) |
|-----------|-------------|
| Known/background info | New/emphasized info |
| "As for X ..." | "It is X that ..." |
| Sets the scene | Answers "who/what?" |

**Example pair:**

- **田中さんは食べた。** (Tanaka-san wa tabeta.) — As for Tanaka, (he) ate. (We already know we're talking about Tanaka.)
- **田中さんが食べた。** (Tanaka-san ga tabeta.) — **Tanaka** is the one who ate. (Answering: "Who ate?")

Another way to see it:

- **この映画は面白い。** (Kono eiga wa omoshiroi.) — This movie (as a topic) is interesting.
- **この映画が面白い。** (Kono eiga ga omoshiroi.) — **This movie** (specifically this one) is interesting.

Over time, you will develop a natural sense for this distinction through practice and exposure.

## Summary

| Particle | Role | Example |
|----------|------|---------|
| は | Topic marker ("As for ...") | 私**は**学生だ |
| も | Inclusive ("also/too") | 私**も**学生だ |
| が | Subject identifier | 誰**が**来た？ |

These three particles are the foundation. In the lessons ahead, you will learn additional particles like を, に, and で, but mastering は, も, and が first will give you the strongest base.
