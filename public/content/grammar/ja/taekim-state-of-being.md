# Declaring Things with だ — The State-of-Being

In English, the verb "to be" is essential: "I **am** a student," "She **is** happy." Japanese handles this very differently. Instead of a separate verb, Japanese attaches a **declarative marker** directly after nouns and な-adjectives. This is your first step into Japanese sentence structure.

## The Declarative だ

The word **だ** is placed after a noun to declare that something is that noun. There is no need for a separate subject pronoun — context handles it.

- **学生だ。** (Gakusei da.) — (I) am a student.
- **元気だ。** (Genki da.) — (I) am healthy/fine.
- **日本人だ。** (Nihonjin da.) — (I/he/she) is Japanese.

> **Note:** In casual conversation, だ is often dropped entirely. Saying just 学生 by itself can mean "I'm a student" in the right context. Women and children especially tend to omit だ in casual speech.

## Negative: じゃない

To say something is **not** something, replace だ with **じゃない**. This is the casual negative form.

- **学生じゃない。** (Gakusei ja nai.) — (I) am not a student.
- **元気じゃない。** (Genki ja nai.) — (I) am not well.
- **静かじゃない。** (Shizuka ja nai.) — (It) is not quiet.

> **Formal alternative:** In polite speech, you may hear **ではない** (de wa nai), which is the more formal version of じゃない.

## Past: だった

To express that something **was** something, use **だった** in place of だ.

- **学生だった。** (Gakusei datta.) — (I) was a student.
- **有名だった。** (Yuumei datta.) — (It) was famous.
- **友達だった。** (Tomodachi datta.) — (We) were friends.

## Negative Past: じゃなかった

To say something **was not** something, use **じゃなかった**.

- **学生じゃなかった。** (Gakusei ja nakatta.) — (I) was not a student.
- **暇じゃなかった。** (Hima ja nakatta.) — (I) was not free/available.
- **簡単じゃなかった。** (Kantan ja nakatta.) — (It) was not simple.

## Conjugation Summary

| Form | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Affirmative | Noun + **だ** | 学生**だ** |
| Negative | Noun + **じゃない** | 学生**じゃない** |
| Past | Noun + **だった** | 学生**だった** |
| Negative past | Noun + **じゃなかった** | 学生**じゃなかった** |

This four-way conjugation pattern is fundamental. You will see it appear again with な-adjectives, since they follow the same だ system.

## Key Takeaways

1. **だ** declares a state-of-being — like "is" in English, but only for nouns and な-adjectives.
2. The negative, past, and negative-past forms follow a consistent pattern.
3. In casual speech, だ can be dropped, but the conjugated forms (じゃない, だった, じゃなかった) are always used.
